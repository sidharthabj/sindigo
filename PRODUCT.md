# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Three overlapping types, all equally important: voracious readers who want a worthy home for their reading life; aspirational readers who want to read more and crave a reading identity to grow into; and social readers who care about what their friends are reading and love books as shared conversation. Ages vary but share a common quality: reading matters to them, and they want that to show.

## Product Purpose

Sindigo is a personal reading tracker made social. Users keep three shelves (Wishlist, Currently Reading, Read), write notes and reviews at any stage, rate books 1-5 (shown as book icons, never stars), follow friends, and see a feed of their friends' reading activity. The product succeeds when a user feels their reading life is beautifully organized and when they discover their next book through someone they trust.

## Brand Personality

Intimate, reflective, personal. Feels like a private reading journal you share with close friends. Quiet confidence, unhurried. Not a utility; a companion. Voice is warm but never chatty, opinionated but never loud. Think: a well-read friend who lends you books with a handwritten note tucked inside.

Reference energy: Letterboxd — personal, slightly moody, has a point of view. Not cold, not dark for its own sake, but confident and considered.

## Anti-references

- **Goodreads**: cluttered, utilitarian, outdated. The default to escape.
- **Duolingo / gamification apps**: no streaks, no badges, no dopamine loops. Reading is not a chore. The book is the reward.
- **Library / academic**: sterile catalogue aesthetic, zero warmth, functional without feeling.
- **Generic SaaS landing**: hero metrics, feature icon grids, pricing comparison tables. None of that.

## Design Principles

1. **The book is the reward.** No gamification language anywhere. Progress and organization are shown as beauty, not as achievement unlocked.
2. **Intimacy first, social second.** The product starts as a personal record and extends into community. The feed feels like being let into someone's reading life, not a social media timeline.
3. **Reading as identity.** Not "what you've finished" as a count — but who you are through what you read. The profile is a portrait, not a ledger.
4. **Confident and considered.** Have a point of view. Don't hedge with neutral grays and vague copy. The product respects readers; it doesn't talk down or over-explain.
5. **Show, don't announce.** Use product images and real UI to demonstrate the experience. No bullet-point feature lists.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. Respect prefers-reduced-motion for all decorative animations. Color should never be the sole carrier of meaning (ratings, shelf status must work without color context).
